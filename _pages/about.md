I am an Assistant Research Professor at the Water Resources Research Institute of Mississippi State University (MSU). I did Master's degree in Weed Science from Tribhuvan University, Nepal, and Ph.D. in Plant and Soil Science from Texas Tech University, USA. I spent more than 10+ years conducting research on agronomic systems, soil water dynamics, evapotranspiration (ET), hydrologic modeling, soil health, and climate-smart technologies. At MSU, my focus is to develop a research program on ecosystem exchange, climate-smart agriculture, greenhouse gas emissions, soil health, and soil-plant-atmospheric modeling. Before joining MSU, I was a Research Scientist at Rodale Institute in Kutztown, PA. At Rodale, my research focus was on cover crop-based agronomic management systems in organic soybean and corn, integrated grain and forage systems, intercropping, and intensification in regenerative organic systems. Previously, I was an Agronomist at USDA-ARS in Stoneville, MS where I worked for a Long-Term Agroecosystem Research (LTAR) project. At ARS, my research evaluated conservational practices in cotton and sorghum cropping systems in the Mississippi Delta. I applied widely applicable tools to quantify soil water balance and ET and utilized remote sensing tools to leverage digital agronomy with hydrologic modeling. I have tested and validated Root Zone Water Quality Model (RZWQM2) with the team of USDA-ARS and MSU for cover crop systems and row crops. I am expanding my research by incorporating advanced technologies to measure greenhouse gases and ET fluxes.

I authored more than 20 peer-reviewed articles related to soil and water conservation in forage and row crop systems, published a book chapter, and synthesized several outreach papers during my career. Currently, I am serving as a co-chair of "Soils and Environmental Quality" division of the ASA-CSSA-SSSA, president of "Scientists and Students of Nepali Origin", and an Executive Member of the "Association of Agricultural Professionals of North America". I have served as an Associate Editor of Agronomy Journal, reviewer of numerous journals, and moderator for conference sessions. I have been serving the scientific community through student mentoring, judging their presentations, and encouraging other members to become leading members of society. I have received the '2025 Conservation Research Award' from the Soil and Water Conservation Society as a recognition for my decade-long dedication to soil and water research.

## Research Projects

<div style="margin-bottom: 1.5rem; padding-bottom: 1.25rem; border-bottom: 1px solid var(--global-divider-color);">
  <h5 style="color: var(--global-theme-color); font-weight: 600; margin-bottom: 0.2rem;">Automated tailwater recirculation in rice to improve water use efficiency and soil health</h5>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">Apr 2026 &ndash; Present &middot; Mississippi State University &middot; USDA-NIFA</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">This USDA-NIFA funded project evaluates the use of automatic tailwater recirculation systems to improve water use efficiency and crop productivity in rice production. The system captures runoff or excess irrigation water from rice fields and automatically pumps it back for reuse, reducing water losses and improving irrigation efficiency. By conserving water, maintaining more consistent field moisture, and reducing nutrient and sediment losses, this approach supports sustainable rice production while helping producers improve yield stability and resource management.</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.35rem;"><a class="btn btn-sm z-depth-0" role="button" href="{{ '/assets/img/img1.jpg' | relative_url }}" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" title="Tailwater recirculation in rice">&#128247; View photo</a></p>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0;"><em>The tailwater recirculation system includes water level sensors, flow meters, automated pumps, control panels, and data loggers to monitor and manage irrigation runoff. The system captures excess water from the field, stores it in a reservoir or ditch, and automatically recirculates it back to the rice field to improve water use efficiency and reduce nutrient and sediment losses.</em></p>
</div>

<div style="margin-bottom: 1.5rem; padding-bottom: 1.25rem; border-bottom: 1px solid var(--global-divider-color);">
  <h5 style="color: var(--global-theme-color); font-weight: 600; margin-bottom: 0.2rem;">Protected N management in corn for productivity and environmental sustainability</h5>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">May 2024 &ndash; Present &middot; Mississippi State University</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">Our nutrient management trial in corn is designed to evaluate how nitrogen application strategies influence crop performance, nitrogen use efficiency, and greenhouse gas emissions. The study examines two nitrogen application rates in combination with different fertilizer placement depths, trench management practices, and nitrification inhibitor treatments. By comparing these management factors, the trial aims to identify strategies that improve nitrogen availability to corn while reducing nitrogen losses through leaching, volatilization, and nitrous oxide emissions. This research will help develop more efficient and environmentally sustainable nitrogen management recommendations for corn production systems.</p>

<div style="margin-bottom: 1.5rem; padding-bottom: 1.25rem; border-bottom: 1px solid var(--global-divider-color);">
  <h5 style="color: var(--global-theme-color); font-weight: 600; margin-bottom: 0.2rem;">Eddy covariance flux research in row rice</h5>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">Jan 2024 &ndash; Present &middot; Mississippi State University &middot; USDA-NRCS</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">This USDA-NRCS funded project on furrow-irrigated rice focuses on developing climate smart rice production strategies that reduce methane emissions and evapotranspiration while maintaining or improving grain yield. The study uses eddy covariance systems to continuously measure field scale carbon, water, and energy fluxes under different row rice management practices. By quantifying methane dynamics, crop water use, and ecosystem responses, this project provides important insight into how row rice systems can improve water use efficiency, reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and support sustainable rice production.</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.35rem;"><a class="btn btn-sm z-depth-0" role="button" href="{{ '/assets/img/img3.jpg' | relative_url }}" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" title="Eddy Covariance System">&#128247; View photo</a></p>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0;"><em>The eddy covariance systems used in this project are equipped with high frequency sensors to continuously measure exchanges of carbon, water vapor, and energy signatures from the crop field. Each tower includes a three dimensional sonic anemometer to measure wind speed and turbulence, along with gas analyzers for monitoring CO&#8322;, H&#8322;O, and CH&#8324; fluxes. Sensors measure air temperature, relative humidity, soil temperature, soil moisture, radiation, precipitation, and other micrometeorological variables.</em></p>
</div>

<div style="margin-bottom: 1.5rem; padding-bottom: 1.25rem; border-bottom: 1px solid var(--global-divider-color);">
  <h5 style="color: var(--global-theme-color); font-weight: 600; margin-bottom: 0.2rem;">Kura clover living mulch for organic crop production</h5>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">Sep 2023 &ndash; Present &middot; Rodale Institute &middot; USDA-NIFA</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">The living mulch project, funded by USDA-NIFA, evaluates the use of kura clover in corn and soybean systems to improve weed suppression, crop productivity, soil health, and water quality. By maintaining a perennial ground cover between cash crop rows, the study aims to reduce weed pressure, limit soil erosion, improve soil structure, enhance nutrient cycling, and reduce nutrient losses through runoff and leaching. This project supports the development of sustainable cropping systems that balance yield, environmental quality, and long term soil resilience.</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.35rem;"><a class="btn btn-sm z-depth-0" role="button" href="{{ '/assets/img/img4.jpg' | relative_url }}" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" title="Strip tillage to plant corn and soy between kura cover strips">&#128247; View photo</a></p>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0;"><em>Strip tillage with kura clover living mulch involves creating narrow tilled strips for corn or soybean planting while maintaining kura clover cover between rows. This system helps suppress weeds, reduce erosion, improve soil structure, enhance nutrient cycling, and protect water quality by limiting runoff and nutrient loss. The photo shows how conservation tillage and perennial ground cover can be integrated to support crop production while improving soil health and environmental sustainability.</em></p>
</div>

<div style="margin-bottom: 1.5rem; padding-bottom: 1.25rem; ">
  <h5 style="color: var(--global-theme-color); font-weight: 600; margin-bottom: 0.2rem;">Maximizing effectiveness of roller-crimping with novel strategies</h5>
  <p style="color: var(--global-text-color-light); font-size: 0.85rem; margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">Sep 2023 &ndash; Present &middot; Rodale Institute &middot; USDA-SARE (Pennsylvania)</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">The roller crimping project, funded by USDA-SARE in Pennsylvania, is designed to evaluate how different crimping directions influence cover crop termination, weed suppression, and crop productivity in sweet corn and hemp systems. The study compares roller crimping approaches across different field directions to determine which orientation provides the most uniform mulch layer, improves soil cover, reduces weed emergence, and supports crop establishment and yield. By integrating mechanical cover crop termination with cash crop production, this project aims to identify practical, herbicide reducing weed management strategies that enhance sustainability and profitability in diversified cropping systems.</p>
</div>
